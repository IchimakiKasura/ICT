CULTURAL VARIATION

  • Cultural variation refers to the rich diversity in social practices that different cultures exhibit around the world. Cuisine and art all change from one culture to the next, but so do gender roles, economic systems, and social hierarchy among any number of other humanly organised behaviours. Cultural differences influences perception by creating lived experiences that teach certain beliefs, values, behaviors, and communication styles. These differences influence the way that people view the world around. Culture considers various aspects of society like language, technology, and norms, whereas society involves people who share a common culture. It is through culture that people understand themselves and relate to societal norms. Therefore, these terms are entirely different but associated.

  • Ethnocentrism compares other cultures by using a group's specific culture as the basis of that comparison, believing theirs to be superior and the standard to be used in comparison to other cultures. Cultural relativism, on the other hand, believes that culture is understood best through its own people. The way cuisines vary across cultures fascinates many people. Some travelers pride themselves on their willingness to try unfamiliar foods, like celebrated food. While others return home expressing gratitude for their native culture’s fare. Often, people in the other countries, express disgust at other cultures’ cuisine and think that it’s gross to eat meat from a dog or guinea pig, for example, while they don’t question their own habit of eating cows or pigs. Such attitudes are an example of ethnocentrism, or evaluating and judging another culture based on how it compares to one’s own cultural norms. Despite how much humans have in common, cultural differences are far more prevalent than cultural universals. For example, while all cultures have language, analysis of particular language structures and conversational etiquette reveal tremendous differences. In some Middle Eastern cultures, it is common to stand close to others in conversation. Ethnocentrism views one's own culture as the standard for comparison, whereas cultural relativism asserts that a culture is best understood through its own people.

    • Cultural variation is vast and can be observed in numerous aspects of different cultures. Here are some examples about the differences of culture variation:
    1. Religious Practices - Different cultures have varying religious beliefs and rituals. For example, the Hindu festival of Diwali in India is distinct from Christmas celebrations in Western cultures.
    2. Cuisine - Food and culinary traditions vary widely, such as the consumption of sushi in Japan, pasta in Italy, or the use of spices in Indian cuisine, and in Philippines they have a delicious Adobo
    3. Language - Languages spoken by different cultures can be vastly different, like Tagalog, Bisaya, Ilocano, Mandarin Chinese, Arabic, Spanish, etc.
    4. Art and Aesthetics - Artistic expressions, like traditional Japanese tea ceremonies, African tribal art, or European Renaissance paintings, showcase cultural variation.
    5. Social Norms - The rules and expectations for social interactions, family structures, and gender roles differ among cultures. For example, arranged marriages in some South Asian cultures contrast with more individualistic Western courtship.
    6. Clothing and Fashion - Traditional clothing like the Filipino street style, the kimono in Japan, the sari in India, or the Scottish kilt represent unique cultural styles.
    7. Festivals and Celebrations - Various cultural events, such as the Fiesta's in Philippines, Carnival in Brazil, Chinese New Year, or the Running of the Bulls in Spain, demonstrate cultural diversity.
    8. Music and Dance - Musical genres like reggae in Jamaica, flamenco in Spain, or the classical sitar music of India are tied to specific cultures, and the Budot's in Philippines
    9. Architectural Styles - Building designs and architectural traditions, such as the pyramids of Egypt, Gothic cathedrals in Europe, or traditional yurts in Mongolia, reflect cultural diversity.
    10. Belief Systems - Philosophies, ideologies, and belief systems, like Confucianism in China, Buddhism in Southeast Asia, or the cultural significance of the American Dream, vary greatly.
    These are just a few examples, and cultural variation can extend to virtually every aspect of human life and society. It's a testament to the richness and complexity of the world's cultures.
